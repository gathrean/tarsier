# Tarsier Lesson Roadmap (v0.1)

> **Last updated:** 2026-03-08  
> **Status:** Draft — pending Ean's QA  
> **Design principle:** Affixes are introduced organically within themed lessons, not in isolation. When a new affix appears for the first time, it's colour-coded and briefly explained in context, then reinforced in subsequent lessons. No lesson is "the mag- lesson" — it's "the food lesson where you happen to learn mag-."

---

## Recurring Lesson Elements

### Colour-Coding System
- When a NEW word or affix appears for the first time, it's highlighted with colour
- Affixes (prefixes, infixes, suffixes): **#6B5B9A** (functional ube purple)
- Root words: **#302B27** (tarsier dark)
- Subsequent appearances fade to normal text (the learner has "learned" it)

### "Alam Mo Ba?" (Did You Know?) Boxes
- Appear whenever a Spanish or American borrowed word is used in Tagalog
- Short, fun, 1-2 sentence cultural/linguistic fact
- Examples: "Alam mo ba? 'Kumusta' comes from the Spanish '¿Cómo está?' — 333 years of Spanish colonisation left hundreds of words in Tagalog."
- Not quizzed — purely enrichment content, adds textbook personality

### Taglish Reality Notes
- Every lesson includes natural Taglish variants for key phrases
- Rule embedded throughout: affixes are always Tagalog, roots/nouns can be English
- Register awareness: when Taglish sounds natural vs when it sounds "conyo"

## Chapter 1: Magalang — Respect & Greetings
**Lessons 1–4 | No affixes yet — pure phrases and social rules**

### Lesson 1: Po & Opo — The Heart of Filipino Respect
- What po/opo means and why it matters
- Po = politeness particle added to any sentence (even English/Taglish: "I didn't eat yet po")
- Opo = "yes" + po (respectful yes)
- Who you use it with: elders, strangers, authority figures, anyone you want to show respect to
- When NOT using po is rude vs when it's fine (friends, same-age peers)
- Po placement: end of sentence, or after the verb ("Kumain na po ako")
- Mano po: the hand-to-forehead blessing gesture with elders
- **Alam mo ba?** "Po" has no direct English translation. It doesn't mean "sir" or "ma'am" — it's a particle that changes the entire tone of a sentence from casual to respectful. There's nothing like it in English."

### Lesson 2: Kumusta — Greetings & Small Talk
- Kumusta po? (How are you?)
- Magandang umaga/tanghali/hapon/gabi po (Good morning/noon/afternoon/evening)
- "Kumain ka na ba?" as a greeting, not a literal question
- Casual vs respectful greetings (kumusta vs kumusta po)
- **Alam mo ba?** "'Kumusta' comes from the Spanish '¿Cómo está?' Filipino absorbed hundreds of Spanish words during 333 years of colonisation."
- **First affix preview (organic):** The word "maganda" appears naturally in greetings. Colour-code it briefly: ma- + ganda (beauty). "You'll see this ma- pattern a lot — it turns root words into descriptions. More on this later."

### Lesson 3: Oo, Hindi, Salamat — Yes, No, Thank You
- Oo / Oo po / Opo (yes, escalating politeness)
- Hindi / Hindi po (no / no, respectfully)
- Salamat / Salamat po / Maraming salamat po
- Pasensya na po (sorry/excuse me — asking patience)
- Walang anuman (you're welcome — "it's nothing")
- Sige / Sige po (okay / okay, respectfully)
- **Alam mo ba?** "'Salamat' may come from the Arabic 'salaam' — a trace of pre-colonial Islamic trade in the Philippines."

### Lesson 4: Ako, Ikaw, Siya — Who's Who
- Personal pronouns: ako (I), ikaw/ka (you), siya (he/she — Tagalog is gender-neutral!)
- Plural: tayo (we, inclusive), kami (we, exclusive), kayo (you all / formal you), sila (they)
- Ko/mo/niya (possessive forms)
- How "po" pairs with pronouns: "kayo" is already respectful (like French "vous"), kayo + po = very formal
- "Sino po sila?" (Who are they?) — practical question
- **Alam mo ba?** "Tagalog has no gendered pronouns — 'siya' means he, she, and they. Filipino speakers sometimes mix up 'he' and 'she' in English because the distinction doesn't exist in Tagalog."

## Chapter 2: Kain! — Food & Basic Verbs
**Lessons 5–8 | Introducing um- infix, mag-/nag- prefixes through food**

### Lesson 5: Kain — Let's Eat
- Root "kain" (eat) — the most important root word in Filipino culture
- kumain (um- infix — first affix taught properly, colour-coded breakdown)
- "Kumain ka na ba?" deep dive — cultural weight, when it's asked, how to respond
- pagkain (food as noun — pag- + kain, colour-coded)
- masarap (delicious) — ma- prefix preview again, reinforcing from Lesson 2
- **Alam mo ba?** "Filipino meals revolve around rice. 'Kanin' (cooked rice) comes from the same root as 'kain' (eat). Eating IS rice."
- Real photos: Filipino rice meal, family eating together

### Lesson 6: Luto — Cooking
- Root "luto" (cook) — the clean mag-/nag- example
- magluto (to cook) / nagluto (cooked) — mag-/nag- prefix taught properly with colour-coded breakdown
- Filipino dish vocabulary: adobo, sinigang, tinola, kare-kare, lechon
- "Si Nanay nag-cook ng sinigang" — Taglish reality (affixes Tagalog, root can be English)
- **Alam mo ba?** "'Adobo' comes from the Spanish 'adobar' (to marinate). But Filipino adobo existed before Spain arrived — the Spanish just gave it a name."
- Real photos: adobo, sinigang, Filipino kitchen

### Lesson 7: Inom — Drinks & Ordering
- Root "inom" (drink) — another um- infix word (uminom)
- inumin (beverage — -in suffix, first appearance, colour-coded briefly)
- kape, tubig, juice — drink vocabulary
- Ordering: "Ano po ang order ninyo?" / "Isa pong kape"
- Counting preview: isa, dalawa, tatlo (just the first three, used in ordering context)
- **Alam mo ba?** "'Tindahan' (store) comes from the Spanish 'tienda.' 'Baso' (glass) comes from 'vaso.' Your kitchen vocabulary is full of Spanish."
- Real photos: Filipino carinderia, 3-in-1 coffee sachets

### Lesson 8: Gusto & Ayaw — Wants & Dislikes
- Gusto ko (I want) / Ayaw ko (I don't want)
- Combining with verbs: "Gusto kong kumain" / "Ayaw kong magluto"
- Po in the middle of sentences: "Gusto ko pong kumain" — po goes after ko, not at the end
- Review of all food verbs learned so far in want/don't want context
- **Alam mo ba?** "'Gusto' is straight from Spanish. The pure Tagalog word is 'ibig' (as in 'ibig sabihin' = meaning), but nobody says 'ibig ko' in daily speech."

## Chapter 3: Pamilya — Family & Relationships
**Lessons 9–12 | Family vocabulary, i- prefix, emotional core for heritage learners**

### Lesson 9: Nanay, Tatay — Immediate Family
- Nanay/Tatay (Mom/Dad — informal: Nay/Tay)
- Kapatid (sibling), anak (child), asawa (spouse)
- Ate (older sister) / Kuya (older brother) / Bunso (youngest)
- **Critical cultural note: Kuya/Ate beyond family**
  - Used for ANY older person as a sign of respect and warmth
  - Calling a street vendor "Kuya" or a coworker "Ate" is normal and expected
  - Parents call their own eldest kids "Kuya" and "Ate" so younger siblings learn the titles early
  - **Age sensitivity:** If you're in your 20s, calling someone in their 30s "Tito/Tita" can feel disrespectful — implies they're a generation older. "Kuya/Ate" is safer and friendlier, especially for women who may be sensitive about age
  - Bunso can be used for the youngest in any group (not just family), though less common
- Taglish reality: many families use "Mom/Dad" and "Kuya/Ate" interchangeably with Nanay/Tatay
- **Alam mo ba?** "'Ate' and 'Kuya' come from Hokkien Chinese (阿姐 a-ché and 哥 ko). Centuries of Chinese trade left these words permanently in Tagalog."

### Lesson 10: Lola, Lolo — Extended Family & Respect
- Lola/Lolo (grandmother/grandfather)
- Tita/Tito (aunt/uncle — but also used for any older adult a generation above you)
- **Tito/Tita vs Kuya/Ate — the age gap rule:**
  - Kuya/Ate = someone older but in your approximate generation (a few years to ~15 years older)
  - Tito/Tita = someone clearly a generation older (your parents' age range)
  - The grey zone (someone 10-15 years older) is where social awareness matters — defaulting to Kuya/Ate is safer
  - Calling a 35-year-old woman "Tita" when you're 25 can land badly — it ages her
- Ninong/Ninang (godfather/godmother — culturally important, not just ceremonial)
- Mano po revisited in family context — when arriving at a family gathering, you mano your elders
- **Alam mo ba?** "'Tita' and 'Tito' come from Spanish 'tía' and 'tío.' Before Spanish colonisation, Tagalog had different kinship terms."

### Lesson 11: Mahal — Love & Affection
- Root "mahal" = both "love" AND "expensive" (context-dependent — a favourite trivia fact)
- Mahal kita (I love you)
- i- prefix introduction (organic): imahal (to love someone/something — directing the action)
- How Filipinos express affection (less verbal "I love you," more acts of service — "Kumain ka na ba?" IS love)
- **Alam mo ba?** "'Mahal' meaning both 'love' and 'expensive' isn't a coincidence — what you love is what you value, and what's valuable is precious. Same root, same idea."

### Lesson 12: Tahanan — Home Life
- Root "bahay" (house) / "tahanan" (home — more poetic)
- Household vocabulary: kusina (kitchen), kwarto (room), banyo (bathroom)
- i- prefix reinforced: "Iluto mo ang adobo" (Cook the adobo — directing the action toward something)
- pa- prefix introduction (organic): "Paluto" (have someone cook for you — causative)
- Basic household commands in family context
- **Alam mo ba?** "'Kusina' = Spanish 'cocina.' 'Kwarto' = Spanish 'cuarto.' 'Banyo' = Spanish 'baño.' 'Bintana' (window) = Spanish 'ventana.' Your house is full of Spanish."

## Chapter 4: Bilang — Counting & Money
**Lessons 13–14 | The three-language counting system, practical numbers**

### Lesson 13: Isa, Dalawa, Tatlo — Filipino Numbers
- Filipino numbers 1–10: isa, dalawa, tatlo, apat, lima, anim, pito, walo, siyam, sampu
- 11–20: labing-isa, labing-dalawa... dalawampu (acknowledge that this gets hard and that's normal)
- Round numbers: tatlumpu (30), limampu (50), isang daan (100), isang libo (1,000)
- Honest admission: almost nobody uses full Filipino numbers past 10 in daily speech
- **Alam mo ba?** "Ask any Filipino to count past 10 in Tagalog and watch them struggle. It's a running joke — 'dalawampu't limang sentimo' has too many syllables for anyone to say at a cash register."

### Lesson 14: Bente, Singkwenta — Money & Spanish Numbers
- The reality: money uses Spanish numbers for common denominations
  - Bente (20, from Spanish "veinte")
  - Trenta (30), kuwarenta (40), singkwenta (50)
  - Bentsingko/benchingko (25 centavos, from "veinticinco")
- English for complex/exact amounts: "one-twenty" not "isang daan at dalawampu"
- Filipino for small coins and round hundreds: isa (1), lima (5), sampu (10), isang daan (100)
- Practical: "Magkano po?" (How much?) — ordering at a carinderia, paying at a palengke
- The code-switching rule: Filipinos pick whichever language has the fewest syllables for that number
- **Alam mo ba?** "Filipinos count in three languages depending on the situation — Filipino for small numbers, Spanish for common prices (bente, singkwenta), and English for anything complex. This isn't confusion — it's efficiency."

## Chapter 5: Araw-araw — Daily Routines
**Lessons 15–18 | Daily actions, -in/-an suffixes introduced organically**

### Lesson 15: Umaga — Morning Routine
- gising (wake up), ligo (bathe/shower), bihis (dress), kain (again — reinforced)
- mag- prefix reinforced with new roots: magligo, magbihis
- Time vocabulary: umaga (morning), tanghali (noon), hapon (afternoon), gabi (night)
- Telling time: Filipino time-telling uses Spanish numbers + Filipino time-of-day markers:
  - "Ala una ng hapon" (1pm), "Alas dos ng hapon" (2pm), "Alas tres ng umaga" (3am)
  - "Alas otso" (8 o'clock), "Alas dose ng tanghali" (12 noon)
  - Half hours: "Alas tres y medya" (3:30 — "y medya" from Spanish "y media")
  - Time-of-day markers: ng madaling-araw (early morning), ng umaga (morning), ng tanghali (noon), ng hapon (afternoon), ng gabi (evening/night), ng hatinggabi (midnight)
  - Beyond simple times, people switch to English: "seven thirty," "quarter to five"
- **Alam mo ba?** "Filipinos tell time in Spanish numbers with Filipino time-of-day words. 'Alas singko ng hapon' = 5pm. The pure Tagalog number system for time ('ikalima ng hapon') exists but almost nobody uses it. Even older Filipinos default to the Spanish system."

### Lesson 16: Trabaho — Work & School
- Root "trabaho" (work) / "aral" (study/learn)
- mag-aral / nag-aral — mag-/nag- reinforced
- nag- with borrowed English words: "nagta-trabaho" (working), "nag-shopping" (went shopping)
- This is the Taglish affix rule made explicit: nag- + any English verb = past tense verb in Taglish
- **Alam mo ba?** "'Trabaho' comes from Spanish 'trabajo.' 'Eskwela' (school) comes from 'escuela.' 'Maestro/maestra' (teacher) is also Spanish — though most Filipinos now say 'teacher.'"

### Lesson 17: Linis & Ayos — Chores & Fixing
- Root "linis" (clean) / "ayos" (fix/arrange)
- -in suffix introduction (organic): linisin (clean IT — the thing being cleaned)
- -an suffix introduction (organic): linisan (clean THE PLACE — the location being cleaned)
- The object-focus system explained simply: -in = the thing, -an = the place/person affected
- "Linisin mo ang kwarto" vs "Linisan mo ang kusina" — same root, different focus
- **Alam mo ba?** "'-in' and '-an' are the suffixes that make Tagalog grammar genuinely different from English. In English you 'clean the room.' In Tagalog, the room becomes the subject: 'Linisan mo ang kwarto' — the room is being cleaned BY you."

### Lesson 18: Pahinga — Rest & Free Time
- Root "pahinga" (rest) / "tulog" (sleep) / "laro" (play)
- pa- prefix reinforced: papahinga (about to rest — future tense)
- naka- prefix (organic): nakatulog (fell asleep accidentally — stative, happened by circumstance)
- Weekend vocabulary: what Filipinos do for fun (malling — yes, it's a verb in Taglish)
- "Nag-mall kami kahapon" — Taglish in action
- **Alam mo ba?** "'Malling' is Filipino English — turning 'mall' into a verb. Filipinos also say 'nag-grocery' (went grocery shopping), 'nag-gym,' and 'nag-Netflix.' If it's an activity, it gets a nag-."

## Chapter 6: Damdamin — Feelings & Descriptions
**Lessons 19–22 | ma- adjective prefix, expressing emotions**

### Lesson 19: Masaya & Malungkot — Happy & Sad
- ma- prefix for adjectives (formally introduced, colour-coded — callback to "maganda" from Lesson 2)
- masaya (happy) / malungkot (sad) / magalit (angry) / matakot (scared)
- Root extraction: lungkot = sadness, takot = fear — strip the prefix to find the root
- "Okay ka lang po?" (Are you okay?) — checking on someone respectfully
- **Alam mo ba?** "Tagalog has specific words for emotions English doesn't capture well. 'Kilig' (the giddy excitement from romance), 'gigil' (the urge to squeeze something cute), and 'tampo' (a silent treatment — not quite anger, not quite sadness)."

### Lesson 20: Maganda & Gwapo — Describing People
- maganda (beautiful) / gwapo (handsome) / mabait (kind) / matalino (smart)
- Describing people respectfully — role of "po" in softening descriptions
- napa- prefix (organic): "Napangiti ako" (I couldn't help but smile) — involuntary actions
- **Alam mo ba?** "'Gwapo' comes from Spanish 'guapo.' 'Bonita' is also used in some regions. Filipino beauty vocabulary is heavily Spanish-influenced."

### Lesson 21: Mainit & Malamig — Weather & Surroundings
- mainit (hot) / malamig (cold) / maulan (rainy) / mahangin (windy)
- Same ma- pattern — root extraction reinforced (init = heat, lamig = cold, ulan = rain, hangin = wind)
- Philippine weather/seasons — only two: tag-ulan (rainy) and tag-init (dry/hot)
- **Alam mo ba?** "'Tag-' is a prefix meaning 'season of.' Tag-ulan = season of rain. Tag-init = season of heat. 'Tag-gutom' (season of hunger) is slang for being broke before payday."

### Lesson 22: Pagod & Gutom — Physical States
- pagod (tired) / gutom (hungry) / uhaw (thirsty) / antok (sleepy)
- Intensifiers: "Pagod na pagod na ako" (super tired), "sobrang gutom" (extremely hungry)
- "Grabe" as the universal Taglish intensifier
- These words don't use ma- — they stand alone as adjectives (noting the exception to the pattern)
- **Alam mo ba?** "'Grabe' comes from Spanish 'grave' (serious/severe). In Taglish, it's evolved into a general intensifier — 'Grabe ang init!' (It's SO hot!), 'Grabe ka!' (You're too much!)."

## Chapter 7: Lugar — Places & Getting Around
**Lessons 23–26 | Location markers, directional affixes, practical navigation**

### Lesson 23: Saan — Where?
- saan (where) / dito (here) / diyan (there, near you) / doon (there, far)
- nasa (location marker — "Nasa bahay ako" = I'm at home)
- "Saan po ito?" (Where is this?) — practical navigation
- "Nasaan ang...?" (Where is the...?)
- **Alam mo ba?** "Tagalog has THREE words for 'there': 'dito' (here, near me), 'diyan' (there, near you), 'doon' (over there, far from both of us). English only has 'here' and 'there.'"

### Lesson 24: Punta — Going Places
- Root "punta" (go to) / "lakad" (walk)
- pumunta (went — um- infix reinforced) / pupunta (will go — future tense pattern)
- papunta (heading toward — pa- prefix as directional)
- Pauwi (heading home — pa- + uwi), palabas (heading out — pa- + labas)
- **Alam mo ba?** "'Lakad' means both 'walk' and 'errand/task' depending on context. 'May lakad ako' = 'I have somewhere to be' (not 'I'm going to walk')."

### Lesson 25: Palengke & Tindahan — Shopping
- Market/store vocabulary: palengke (wet market), tindahan (store), mall
- Counting reinforced: numbers used for prices
- "Magkano po?" (How much?) — the essential shopping phrase
- "Mura" (cheap) / "Mahal" (expensive — callback to Lesson 11's double meaning)
- Bargaining basics: "Tawad po" (discount please)
- **Alam mo ba?** "'Palengke' comes from the Spanish 'palenque' (a fenced area). 'Tindahan' has the Spanish root 'tienda.' Shopping vocabulary is one of the most Spanish-influenced areas of Tagalog."

### Lesson 26: Sakay — Transportation
- Jeepney/tricycle/bus/MRT vocabulary
- **"Para po!"** — the most iconic po usage (calling out for the jeepney to stop)
- sakay (ride/board) / baba (get off) — root words
- "Saan po kayo bababa?" (Where will you get off?) — baba with -an suffix preview
- Bayad po (payment — what you say when passing money forward in a jeepney)
- **Alam mo ba?** "'Jeepney' comes from American military jeeps left after WWII. Filipinos modified them into public transport and decorated them — now they're a national icon."

## Chapter 8: Oras — Time & Planning
**Lessons 27–28 | Tense consolidation, making plans**

### Lesson 27: Kahapon, Ngayon, Bukas — Time & Tenses
- Time markers: kahapon (yesterday), ngayon (now/today), bukas (tomorrow), kanina (earlier), mamaya (later)
- How tense prefixes map: nag- = kahapon, nag-_-_ = ngayon, mag- = bukas
- Consolidation: all affixes learned so far, shown in a "tense table" for familiar roots
- "Kahapon nag-cook ako, ngayon nagluluto ako, bukas mag-cook ulit ako" — tense in Taglish

### Lesson 28: Plano — Making Plans
- "Tara!" (Let's go!) — the universal invitation word
- "Pwede po ba...?" (May I...? / Is it okay if...?) — asking permission politely
- Making invitations: "Sama ka?" (Wanna come along?)
- Scheduling in Taglish: "Free ka ba this Saturday?"
- How Filipinos say no without saying no: "Sige, tignan ko" (Okay, I'll see), "Baka pwede next time"
- "Sa susunod" (next time) — the polite decline
- **Alam mo ba?** "'Pwede' comes from Spanish 'puede.' 'Sige' may come from Spanish 'sigue' (go on/continue). Even your agreement words are Spanish."

## Chapter 9: Kwento — Putting It All Together
**Lessons 29–30 | Stacking affixes, the "word machine," heritage connection**

### Lesson 29: Salita — The Word Machine
- THE Pacquiao lesson
- How ANY word becomes a verb with affixes: "pinacquiao ko yung exam" (I Pacquiao'd the exam)
- Real examples: na-traffic (got stuck in traffic), nag-shopping, nag-Netflix, pine-friendzone
- The "decode any word" framework: see an unfamiliar Tagalog word → strip the affix → find the root → understand the meaning
- Stacking affixes: pina- + luto = pinaluto (had someone cook something — causative past tense)
- magpa- + luto = magpaluto (to have someone cook — causative future)
- This is the moment where the affix system "clicks" — one root → infinite usable words
- **Alam mo ba?** "This affix system is why Duolingo hasn't added Tagalog — it's too structurally different from European languages for their template. But it's also why, once you understand it, you can decode words you've NEVER seen before."

### Lesson 30: Tagalog Mo — Your Tagalog
- Review of all roots and affixes learned across 30 lessons
- A "build your own sentence" exercise — user picks a root, picks affixes, constructs meaning
- The heritage reconnection moment: "You now have the tools to understand words you've never studied. The next time you hear your lola speak, listen for the roots."
- Call to action: continue learning with AI practice, or revisit any chapter
- If Baybayin module exists by this point, tease it: "Want to write your name the way your ancestors did?"
